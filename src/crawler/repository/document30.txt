      <p><b>Dog breeds</b> are <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> that have relatively uniform physical characteristics developed by humans, with breeding animals selected for <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypic</a> traits such as size, coat color, structure, and behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Cynologique_Internationale" title="Fédération Cynologique Internationale">Fédération Cynologique Internationale</a> recognizes 337 pure dog breeds. </p>
      <p>Other uses of the term breed when referring to dogs may include <a href="/wiki/Purebred_dog" title="Purebred dog">pure breeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dog_crossbreed" title="Dog crossbreed">cross-breeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mixed_breed_dog" class="mw-redirect" title="Mixed breed dog">mixed breeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landrace#dogs" title="Landrace">natural breeds</a>. </p>

      <p>The domestic dog is the first species, and the only large <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivore</a>, to have been domesticated. Over the past 200 years, dogs have undergone rapid <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypic</a> change and were formed into today's modern breeds due to <a href="/wiki/Artificial_selection" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial selection">artificial selection</a> imposed by humans. These breeds can vary in size and weight from a 0.46&nbsp;kg (1.0&nbsp;lb) teacup <a href="/wiki/Poodle" title="Poodle">poodle</a> to a 90&nbsp;kg (200&nbsp;lb) giant <a href="/wiki/Mastiff" title="Mastiff">mastiff</a>. The skull, body, and limb proportions vary significantly between breeds, with dogs displaying more phenotypic diversity than can be found within the entire order of carnivores. Some breeds demonstrate outstanding skills in herding, retrieving, scent detection, and guarding, which demonstrates the functional and behavioral diversity of dogs. The first dogs were certainly wolflike, but the phenotypic changes that coincided with the dog–wolf <a href="/wiki/Genetic_divergence" title="Genetic divergence">genetic divergence</a> are not known.<sup id="cite_ref-freedman2017_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freedman2017-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 2017, a study showed that 9,000 years ago the domestic dog was present at what is now <a href="/wiki/Zhokhov_Island" title="Zhokhov Island">Zhokhov Island</a>, arctic north-eastern Siberia, which was connected to the mainland at that time. The dogs were selectively bred as either sled dogs or as hunting dogs, which implies that a sled dog standard and a hunting dog standard existed at that time. The optimal maximum size for a sled dog is 20–25&nbsp;kg (44–55&nbsp;lb) based on thermo-regulation, and the ancient sled dogs were between 16–25&nbsp;kg (35–55&nbsp;lb). The same standard has been found in the remains of sled dogs from this region 2,000 ago and in the modern <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Husky" title="Siberian Husky">Siberian Husky</a> breed standard. Other dogs were more massive at 30&nbsp;kg (66&nbsp;lb) and appear to be dogs that had been crossed with wolves and used for polar-bear hunting. At death, the heads of the dogs had been carefully separated from their bodies by humans, probably for ceremonial reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-pitulko2017_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitulko2017-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>Between 3,000 and 4,000 years ago greyhound-type dogs were depicted on pottery and paintings in Egypt and Western Asia. Mastiff-type dogs were kept for guarding and hunting, and short-legged dogs were also bred.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Most modern dog breeds are the products of the controlled breeding practices of the Victorian era (1830-1900),<sup id="cite_ref-Atlas_of_Dog_Breeds_of_the_World_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas_of_Dog_Breeds_of_the_World-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-American_Kennel_Club_2006_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Kennel_Club_2006-6">[6]</a></sup> and the accurate documenting of pedigrees with the establishment of the English Kennel Club in 1873 in imitation of other stud book registries for cattle and horses.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_dog_breeds">Ancient dog breeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient dog breeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Ancient breed of dogs was a term once used for a group of dog breeds by the American Kennel Club,<sup id="cite_ref-American_Kennel_Club_2006_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Kennel_Club_2006-6">[6]</a></sup> but no longer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These breeds were referred to as "ancient breeds", as opposed to modern breeds because historically it was believed that they had origins dating back over 500 years. In 2004, a study looked at the <a href="/wiki/Microsatellite#Analysis_of_microsatellites" title="Microsatellite">microsatellites</a> of 414 purebred dogs representing 85 breeds. The study found that dog breeds were so genetically distinct that 99% of individual dogs could be correctly assigned to their breed based on their <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a>, indicating that breeding barriers (pure-bred breeding) has led to distinct genetic units. The study identified 9 breeds that could be represented on the branches of a <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a> which grouped together with strong statistical support and could be separated from the other breeds with a modern European origin. These 9 breeds had been referred to as "ancient breeds", as opposed to the modern breeds. The study found that the <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh_Hound" title="Pharaoh Hound">Pharaoh Hound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ibizan_Hound" title="Ibizan Hound">Ibizan Hound</a> were not as old as believed but had been recreated from combinations of other breeds, and that the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Elkhound" title="Norwegian Elkhound">Norwegian Elkhound</a> grouped with the other European dogs despite reports of direct Scandinavian origins dating back 5,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-parker2004_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parker2004-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The dog which accompanied the ancestors of Native Americans from Siberia is extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-Science7618_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science7618-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UI7518_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UI7518-11">[11]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dog_types">Dog types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dog types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a href="/wiki/File:Five_different_types_of_dogs,_woodcut,_1547_Wellcome_L0029217.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Five_different_types_of_dogs%2C_woodcut%2C_1547_Wellcome_L0029217.jpg/220px-Five_different_types_of_dogs%2C_woodcut%2C_1547_Wellcome_L0029217.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Five_different_types_of_dogs%2C_woodcut%2C_1547_Wellcome_L0029217.jpg/330px-Five_different_types_of_dogs%2C_woodcut%2C_1547_Wellcome_L0029217.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Five_different_types_of_dogs%2C_woodcut%2C_1547_Wellcome_L0029217.jpg/440px-Five_different_types_of_dogs%2C_woodcut%2C_1547_Wellcome_L0029217.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2982" data-file-height="3852"></a>

      <p>The spread of modern dog breeds has been difficult to resolve because many are the products of the controlled breeding practices of the Victorian era (1830–1900).<sup id="cite_ref-Atlas_of_Dog_Breeds_of_the_World_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atlas_of_Dog_Breeds_of_the_World-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-American_Kennel_Club_2006_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Kennel_Club_2006-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2010, a study looked at 48,000&nbsp;<a href="/wiki/SNP_genotyping" title="SNP genotyping">single nucleotide polymorphisms</a> that gave a <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>-wide coverage of 912&nbsp;dogs representing 85&nbsp;breeds.<sup id="cite_ref-vonHoldt_etal_2010_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vonHoldt_etal_2010-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The study found distinct genetic clusters within modern dogs that largely corresponded to <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a> or function. These included spitz-breeds, toy dogs, spaniels, Mastiff-like breeds, small terriers, retrievers, herding dogs, scent-hounds, and sight-hounds. There were 17&nbsp;breeds that conflicted with phenotype or function and these were thought to be the result of crossing some of the other phenotypes. As in a 2004 study that found 9&nbsp;‘ancient breeds’ to be genetically divergent, the study found 13&nbsp;breeds that were genetically divergent from the modern breeds: the <a href="/wiki/Basenji" title="Basenji">Basenji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saluki" title="Saluki">Saluki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghan_hound" class="mw-redirect" title="Afghan hound">Afghan hound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samoyed_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Samoyed (dog)">Samoyed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canaan_dog" class="mw-redirect" title="Canaan dog">Canaan dog</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_singing_dog" title="New Guinea singing dog">New Guinea singing dog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dingo" title="Dingo">dingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chow_Chow" title="Chow Chow">Chow Chow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Shar_Pei" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Shar Pei">Chinese Shar Pei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akita_(dog)" title="Akita (dog)">Akita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaskan_malamute" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaskan malamute">Alaskan malamute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberian_husky" class="mw-redirect" title="Siberian husky">Siberian husky</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Eskimo_dog" class="mw-redirect" title="American Eskimo dog">American Eskimo dog</a>. Results also indicated that the <a href="/wiki/Basenji" title="Basenji">Basenji</a> had recent admixture with Middle Eastern wolves.<sup id="cite_ref-vonHoldt_etal_2010_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vonHoldt_etal_2010-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The study found that there were three well-supported groups that were highly divergent and distinct from modern domestic dogs. The three groups were </p> 
      <ul>
       <li>an <i>Asian group</i> (<a href="/wiki/Dingo" title="Dingo">Dingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_singing_dog" title="New Guinea singing dog">New Guinea singing dog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chow_chow" class="mw-redirect" title="Chow chow">chow chow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akita_(dog)" title="Akita (dog)">Akita</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shar_Pei" title="Shar Pei">Shar Pei</a>) that showed past admixture with Chinese wolves,</li> 
       <li>a <i>Middle Eastern</i> group (<a href="/wiki/Afghan_hound" class="mw-redirect" title="Afghan hound">Afghan hound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saluki" title="Saluki">Saluki</a>), and</li> 
       <li>a <i>northern group</i> (<a href="/wiki/Alaskan_Malamute" title="Alaskan Malamute">Alaskan Malamute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Husky" title="Siberian Husky">Siberian Husky</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-vonHoldt_etal_2010_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vonHoldt_etal_2010-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
      </ul> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basal_breeds">Basal breeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Basal breeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 2012, a study looked at 49,000 <a href="/wiki/SNP_genotyping" title="SNP genotyping">Single nucleotide polymorphisms</a> that gave a <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>-wide coverage of 1,375 dogs representing 35 breeds, 19 wolves, and previous published genetic signatures of other breeds, giving a total of 121 breeds covered. The study found a deep genetic split between old-world and new-world wolves, and confirmed the genetic divergence of 13 breeds from a 2010 study (<a href="/wiki/Afghan_Hound" title="Afghan Hound">Afghan Hound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akita_(dog)" title="Akita (dog)">Akita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaskan_Malamute" title="Alaskan Malamute">Alaskan Malamute</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Eskimo" class="mw-redirect" title="American Eskimo">American Eskimo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basenji" title="Basenji">Basenji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canaan_dog" class="mw-redirect" title="Canaan dog">Canaan dog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chow_Chow" title="Chow Chow">Chow Chow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dingo" title="Dingo">Dingo</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea_singing_dog" title="New Guinea singing dog">New Guinea singing dog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saluki" title="Saluki">Saluki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samoyed_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Samoyed (dog)">Samoyed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shar-Pei" class="mw-redirect" title="Shar-Pei">Shar-Pei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Husky" title="Siberian Husky">Siberian Husky</a>), plus another 3: the <a href="/wiki/Eurasier" title="Eurasier">Eurasier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Spitz" title="Finnish Spitz">Finnish Spitz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shiba_Inu" title="Shiba Inu">Shiba Inu</a>. The study referred to these 16 as <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">Basal</a> breeds, as opposed to ancient breeds, as they exhibited genetic divergence but not all of them were historically considered to be "ancient breeds". </p>
      <p>The 2012 study found that modern breeds only emerged in the 19th century and that claims of their antiquity are based on little or no historical or empirical evidence. The study indicated that throughout history, global dog populations experienced numerous episodes of diversification and homogenization, with each round further reducing the power of genetic data derived from modern breeds to help infer their early history. </p>
      <p>Of the basal breeds, the <a href="/wiki/American_Eskimo_Dog" title="American Eskimo Dog">American Eskimo Dog</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasier" title="Eurasier">Eurasier</a> were the very recent product of cross-breeding other basal breeds. Most basal breeds have hybridized with other lineages in the past. If those other lineages were other basal breeds then a basal genetic signature remains. The combination of introgression and past population bottlenecks suggested that basal breeds have little or no genetic connections to their ancestral populations and that their genetic distinctiveness does not signify ancient heritage. They are distinctive from the modern breeds because the genetic heritage of the modern breeds has become blurred due to admixture, and the basal breeds have mostly avoided admixture with them due to geographic or cultural barriers.<sup id="cite_ref-larson2012_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-larson2012-13">[13]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_research">Medical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Medical research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>As dogs are a subspecies but their breeds are distinct genetic units, and because only certain breeds share the same type of cancers as humans, the differences in the genes of different breeds may be useful in human medical research.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breed_temperament">Breed temperament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breed temperament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>In 2014, a study indicated that some breed-temperaments, such as anxiety or fear, may be linked to gene mutations. Other temperaments may be due to the legacies of 'ancient' ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> </p>

      <p>Groups of owners that have dogs of the same breed and have an interest in <a href="/wiki/Dog_breeding" title="Dog breeding">dog breeding</a> can form national <a href="/wiki/Kennel_clubs" class="mw-redirect" title="Kennel clubs">Kennel clubs</a>. Kennel Clubs maintain <a href="/wiki/Breed_standard" title="Breed standard">breed standards</a>, record <a href="/wiki/Pedigree_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedigree (dog)">pedigrees</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Breed_registry" title="Breed registry">breed registry</a> (or studbook), and issue the rules for <a href="/wiki/Conformation_dog_show" class="mw-redirect" title="Conformation dog show">conformation dog shows</a> and trials and accreditation of judges. They often serve as <a href="/wiki/Breed_registry" title="Breed registry">registries</a>, which are lists of adult <a href="/wiki/Purebred" title="Purebred">purebred</a> dogs and lists of litters of puppies born to purebred parents. </p>
      <p>A dog breed is represented by a sufficient number of individuals to stably transfer its specific characteristics over generations. Dogs of same <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breed</a> have similar characteristics of appearance and behavior, primarily because they come from a select set of ancestors who had the same characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-{{ISBN|0764573020}}_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-{{ISBN|0764573020}}-16">[16]</a></sup> Dogs of a specific breed <a href="/wiki/Breed_true" class="mw-redirect" title="Breed true">breed true</a>, producing young that are very similar to their parents. An individual dog is identified as a member of a breed through proof of ancestry, using genetic analysis or written records of ancestry. Without such proof, identification of a specific breed is not reliable.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Such records, called <a href="/wiki/Stud_book" class="mw-redirect" title="Stud book">stud books</a>, may be maintained by individuals, clubs, or other organizations. </p>
      <p>Kennel clubs provide the recognition of distinct dog breeds, but there are many independent clubs with differing, and sometimes inconsistent standards and they need not apply scientific standards. Four varieties of the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Shepherd_Dog" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian Shepherd Dog">Belgian Shepherd Dog</a> are recognised as four distinct breeds by the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Kennel_Club" title="New Zealand Kennel Club">New Zealand Kennel Club</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Further, some groups of dogs which clearly share a persistent set of characteristics and documented descent from a known foundation stock may still not be recognized by some clubs as breeds. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Feist_(dog)" title="Feist (dog)">feist</a> is a hunting dog raised in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a> for hunting small game. Feists have a consistent set of characteristics that reliably differentiate them from other <a href="/wiki/Dog_type" title="Dog type">dog types</a> and breeds. However, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kennel_Club" title="United Kennel Club">United Kennel Club</a> recognizes one breed of feist, the <a href="/wiki/Treeing_Feist" class="mw-redirect" title="Treeing Feist">Treeing Feist</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a> does not recognize any feist breed. </p>
      <p>A dog is said to be <a href="/wiki/Purebred_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Purebred (dog)">purebred</a> if their parents were purebred and if the dog meets the standards of the breed. Purebred dog breeders of today "have inherited a breeding paradigm that is, at the very least, a bit anachronistic in light of modern genetic knowledge, and that first arose out of a pretty blatant misinterpretation of Darwin and an enthusiasm for social theories that have long been discredited as scientifically insupportable and morally questionable."<sup id="cite_ref-Budiansky_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Budiansky-19">[19]</a></sup> Morally questionable policies regarding purity of breed include obligatory surgical procedures to spay or neuter animals in numerous contexts. The American Kennel Club, for instance, allows <a href="/wiki/Mixed-breed_dog" class="mw-redirect" title="Mixed-breed dog">mixed-breed dogs</a> to be shown but requires these animals to be altered. It doesn't make such requirements for purebred dogs. California Assembly Act AB 1634 was a bill introduced in 2007 that would require all non-working dogs of mixed breed over the age of 6 months to be neutered or spayed.<sup id="cite_ref-ab1634_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ab1634-20">[20]</a></sup> The bill was morally controversial, leading the American Kennel Club to fight the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Canadian department of agriculture has strict standards for the documenting of what it calls "evolving breeds".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Breed_standards">Breed standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Breed standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/Breed_standard_(dogs)" class="mw-redirect" title="Breed standard (dogs)">breed standard</a> for each breed of dog is a detailed description of the appearance and behaviour of an idealized dog of that breed.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Included in the breed standard description are externally observable aspects of appearance and behaviour that are considered by the breed club to be the most important for the breed, and externally observable details of appearance or temperament that are considered by the breed club to be unacceptable (called <i><a href="/wiki/Fault_(dog)" title="Fault (dog)">faults</a></i>). In addition, most breed standards include a historical section, describing the place of origin and the original work done by the breed or its ancestor <a href="/wiki/Dog_type" title="Dog type">types</a>. </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_registries">Major registries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Major registries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>Dogs with a breed standard may be accepted into one or more of the major registries (<a href="/wiki/Kennel_club" title="Kennel club">kennel clubs</a>) of dog breeds include the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Cynologique_Internationale" title="Fédération Cynologique Internationale">Fédération Cynologique Internationale</a> (covering 84 countries), <a href="/wiki/The_Kennel_Club" title="The Kennel Club">The Kennel Club</a> (UK), the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Kennel_Club" title="Canadian Kennel Club">Canadian Kennel Club</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kennel_Clubs_International" title="United Kennel Clubs International">United Kennel Clubs International</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_Kennel_Council" title="Australian National Kennel Council">Australian National Kennel Council</a> the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Kennel_Club" title="New Zealand Kennel Club">New Zealand Kennel Club</a>, and other national registries. The registry places the breed into the appropriate category, called a <a href="/wiki/Breed_groups_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Breed groups (dog)">group</a>. Some Groups may be further subdivided by some registries. </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_issues">Health issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Health issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>Pure breed dogs have more health problems than <a href="/wiki/Mongrel" title="Mongrel">mongrel</a> dogs, and require more veterinary visits,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and tend to have lower longevity.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Indeed, studies have reported lifespans that are shorter by between one and almost two years.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Notably, dog breeds with flat faces and short noses have breathing difficulties,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> eye trouble and other health issues.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_pure_breeds">List of pure breeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: List of pure breeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4> 
      <p>The <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Cynologique_Internationale" title="Fédération Cynologique Internationale">Fédération Cynologique Internationale</a> recognizes over 300 dog breeds. </p>

      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed-breeds">Mixed-breeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mixed-breeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

      <p>A mongrel, mixed-breed dog or mutt is a <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dog</a> that does not belong to one officially recognized breed and is not the result of intentional <a href="/wiki/Dog_breeding" title="Dog breeding">breeding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ultimate_Morris_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ultimate_Morris-32">[32]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_breeds">Natural breeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Natural breeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Landrace#Dogs" title="Landrace">Natural breeds</a> rose through time in response to a particular environment and in isolation from other populations of the species.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This environment included humans but with little or no <a href="/wiki/Dog_breeding" title="Dog breeding">selective breeding by humans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Groups_of_dogs_mistaken_for_breeds">Groups of dogs mistaken for breeds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_breed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Groups of dogs mistaken for breeds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Dog types are broad categories of dogs based on form, function or style of work, lineage, or appearance. In contrast, modern dog breeds are particular breed standards, sharing a common set of heritable characteristics, determined by the kennel club that recognizes the breed. Examples include the huntaway and other livestock dogs of New Zealand, the <a href="/wiki/Feist_(dog)" title="Feist (dog)">feist dogs</a> of the southern United States, and the Patagonian sheepdogs of Argentina, which are collies mixed with other working dogs.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> </p>
