   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Before Present</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">

    </div>

    <div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       "YBP" and "ybp" redirect here. For the airport with the IATA code YBP, see

      <p><b>Before Present</b> (<b>BP</b>) years is a <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">time scale</a> used mainly in <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific</a> disciplines to specify when events occurred in the past. Because the "present" time changes, standard practice is to use 1 January 1950 as the commencement date of the age scale, reflecting the origin of practical <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> in the 1950s. The abbreviation "BP" has alternatively been interpreted as "Before Physics";<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> that is, before <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_testing" title="Nuclear weapons testing">nuclear weapons testing</a> artificially altered the proportion of the <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_carbon" title="Isotopes of carbon">carbon isotopes</a> in the atmosphere, making dating after that time likely to be unreliable.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In a convention that is not always observed, many sources restrict the use of BP dates to those produced with radiocarbon dating. </p>

      <h2><span id="Usage_of_.22BP.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_&quot;BP&quot;">Usage of "BP"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Before_Present&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage of &quot;BP&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The BP scale is sometimes used for dates established by means other than radiocarbon dating, such as <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AGUStyle_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AGUStyle-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Strat_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Strat-5">[5]</a></sup> This usage differs with the recommendation by van der Plicht &amp; Hogg,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> followed by the <i>Quaternary Science Reviews</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> both of which requested that publications should use the unit "<a href="/wiki/Annum" class="mw-redirect" title="Annum">a</a>" for year and reserve the term "BP" for radiocarbon estimations. </p>
      <p>Some <a href="/wiki/Archaeologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeologist">archaeologists</a> use the lowercase letters <i>bp</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Before_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Before Christ">bc</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">ad</a></i> as terminology for uncalibrated dates for these eras.<sup id="cite_ref-Huth1994_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huth1994-9">[9]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The Centre for Ice and Climate at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Copenhagen" title="University of Copenhagen">University of Copenhagen</a> has proposed <b>"b2k"</b> as "years before AD 2000", based on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenland_Ice_Core_Chronology_2005&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Greenland Ice Core Chronology 2005 (page does not exist)">Greenland Ice Core Chronology 2005</a> (GICC05) time scale.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiocarbon_dating">Radiocarbon dating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Before_Present&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Radiocarbon dating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Radiocarbon dating was first used in 1940. Beginning in 1954, <a href="/wiki/Metrologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Metrologist">metrologists</a> established 1950 as the origin year for the BP scale for use with <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a>, using a 1950-based reference sample of <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>. According to scientist A. Currie Lloyd: </p> 
      <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>The problem was tackled by the international radiocarbon community in the late 1950s, in cooperation with the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards" class="mw-redirect" title="National Bureau of Standards">National Bureau of Standards</a>. A large quantity of contemporary <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a> <a href="/wiki/Dihydrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Dihydrate">dihydrate</a> was prepared as NBS Standard Reference Material (SRM) 4990B. Its <sup>14</sup>C concentration was about 5% above what was believed to be the natural level, so the standard for radiocarbon dating was defined as 0.95 times the <sup>14</sup>C concentration of this material, adjusted to a <sup>13</sup>C reference value of ?19 per mil (PDB). This value is defined as "modern carbon" referenced to AD 1950. Radiocarbon measurements are compared to this modern carbon value, and expressed as "fraction of modern" (fM). "Radiocarbon ages" are calculated from fM using the exponential decay relation and the "Libby half-life" 5568 a. The ages are expressed in years before present (BP) where "present" is defined as AD 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-Currie_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Currie-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
      </blockquote> 
      <p>The year 1950 was chosen because it was the standard <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_epoch" class="mw-redirect" title="Astronomical epoch">astronomical epoch</a> at that time. It also marked<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">[2]</a></sup> the publication of the first radiocarbon dates in December 1949,<sup id="cite_ref-Arnold_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold-12">[12]</a></sup> and 1950 also antedates large scale <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_testing" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear testing">atmospheric testing of nuclear weapons</a>, which altered the global ratio of <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carbon-12" title="Carbon-12">carbon-12</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> </p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiocarbon_calibration">Radiocarbon calibration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Before_Present&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radiocarbon calibration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: 
       <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating#Calibration" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating §&nbsp;Calibration</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Dates determined using radiocarbon dating come in two kinds: <i>uncalibrated</i> (also called <i>Libby</i> or <i>raw</i>) and <i>calibrated</i> (also called <i>Cambridge</i>) dates.<sup id="cite_ref-Archaeology_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Archaeology-14">[14]</a></sup> <i>Uncalibrated</i> radiocarbon dates should be clearly noted as such by "uncalibrated years BP", because they are not identical to calendar dates. This has to do with the fact that the level of atmospheric radiocarbon (<a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a> or <sup>14</sup>C) has not been strictly constant during the span of time that can be radiocarbon-dated. Uncalibrated radiocarbon ages can be converted to calendar dates by means of <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating#Calibration" title="Radiocarbon dating">calibration curves</a> based on comparison of raw radiocarbon dates of samples independently dated by other methods, such as <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a> (dating on the basis of tree growth-rings) and <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a> (dating on the basis of sediment layers in mud or sedimentary rock). Such calibrated dates are expressed as cal BP, where "cal" indicates "calibrated years", or "calendar years", before 1950. </p>
      <p>Many scholarly/scientific journals require that published calibrated results be accompanied by the name (standard codes are used) of the laboratory concerned, and other information such as confidence levels, because of differences between the methods used by different laboratories and changes in calibrating methods. </p>
