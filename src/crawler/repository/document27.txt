            <p>For the Holocene, dates are relative to the year 2000 (e.g. Greenlandian began 11,700 years before 2000). For the begin of the Northgrippian a date of 8,236 years before 2000 has been set.<sup id="cite_ref-IUGS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IUGS-2">[2]</a></sup> The Meghalayan has been set to begin 4,250 years before 2000, apparently from a calibrated radio-carbon date of 4,200 years <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> i.e. before 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-chart_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chart-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Holocene ages/stages only? Or all Quaternary ages/stages? (July 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> </p>
            <p>'Chibanian' and 'Tarantian' are informal, unofficial names proposed to replace the also informal, unofficial 'Middle&nbsp;Pleistocene' and 'Upper&nbsp;Pleistocene' subseries/subepochs respectively. </p>
            <p>In Europe and North America, the Holocene is subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Preboreal" title="Preboreal">Preboreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boreal_(period)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boreal (period)">Boreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(period)" title="Atlantic (period)">Atlantic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subboreal" title="Subboreal">Subboreal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Subatlantic" title="Subatlantic">Subatlantic</a> stages of the <a href="/wiki/Blytt%E2%80%93Sernander_system" title="BlyttSernander system">BlyttSernander time scale</a>. There are many regional subdivisions for the Upper or Late Pleistocene; usually these represent locally recognized cold (<a href="/wiki/Glacial" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial">glacial</a>) and warm (<a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a>) periods. The <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a> ends with the cold <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> substage. </p>

      <p>The <b>Late Pleistocene</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Age_(geology)" title="Age (geology)">geochronological age</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">Epoch</a> and is associated with <b>Upper Pleistocene</b> (or <b>Tarantian</b>) <a href="/wiki/Stage_(stratigraphy)" title="Stage (stratigraphy)">stage</a> rocks. The beginning of the stage is defined by the base of the <a href="/wiki/Eemian_interglacial" class="mw-redirect" title="Eemian interglacial">Eemian interglacial</a> phase before the final glacial episode of the Pleistocene 126,000 ± 5,000 years ago. Its end is defined at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>, some 11,700 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWalkerJohnsenRasmussenPopp20093_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWalkerJohnsenRasmussenPopp20093-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The age represents the end of the Pleistocene epoch and is followed by the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> epoch. </p>
      <p>Much of the Late Pleistocene age was dominated by glaciations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_glaciation" title="Wisconsin glaciation">Wisconsin glaciation</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Weichselian_glaciation" title="Weichselian glaciation">Weichselian glaciation</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrm_glaciation" title="Würm glaciation">Würm glaciation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>). Many <a href="/wiki/Megafauna" title="Megafauna">megafauna</a> became <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_extinction_event" title="Quaternary extinction event">extinct</a> during this age, a trend that continued into the Holocene. The Late Pleistocene contains the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a> stage of human development, including the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Dispersal" title="Southern Dispersal">out-of-Africa</a> migration and <a href="/wiki/Early_human_migrations" title="Early human migrations">dispersal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Anatomically_modern_humans" class="mw-redirect" title="Anatomically modern humans">anatomically modern humans</a> and the extinction of the last remaining <a href="/wiki/Archaic_human" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaic human">archaic human</a> species. </p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Pleistocene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>According to <a href="/wiki/George_Carr_Frison" title="George Carr Frison">George Carr Frison</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bison_occidentalis" title="Bison occidentalis">Bison occidentalis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bison_antiquus" title="Bison antiquus">Bison antiquus</a></i>, an extinct subspecies of the smaller present-day bison, survived the Late Pleistocene period, between about 12 and 11 <a href="/wiki/Kiloannum" class="mw-redirect" title="Kiloannum">ka</a> ago. Plains and Rocky Mountain First Nations depended on these bison as their major food source.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrison2000_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrison2000-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[Notes 1]</a></sup> Earlier kills of camels, horses, and muskoxen found at <a href="/wiki/Wally%27s_beach" class="mw-redirect" title="Wally's beach">Wally's beach</a> were dated to 13.113.3 ka B.P.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> </p>
